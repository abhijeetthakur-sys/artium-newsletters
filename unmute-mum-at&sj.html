We tried it with our students first. A bossanova reinterpretation of Tum Ho leading into Khaabon Ke Parindey. The idea was there. The arrangement was there. But something about the execution kept missing. Eventually we just got up and did it ourselves.

It was a filler meant to fill time during the Mumbai unMute Live episode while other things came together. A small stage, a small audience. Nobody was planning to keep it. Then we heard the playback and the recording, and absolutely loved it.

Tum Ho is the kind of song people hold onto. A.R. Rahman wrote it for Rockstar, Mohit Chauhan sang it, and it found its way into a very specific corner of people's memories. Reinterpreting something like that is a risk. A bossanova rhythm doesn't erase what the original already is. It just approaches the same melody from somewhere else. Whether that works for you depends entirely on how tightly you're holding the original.

Khaabon Ke Parindey was the natural next step. Javed Akhtar wrote it for Zindagi Na Milegi Dobara, performed originally on a rooftop in Spain. A song about the things you keep almost starting. Next to Tum Ho it makes an odd kind of sense. One is about longing for someone. The other is about longing for a version of yourself. The same rhythm carries both.

Tum Ho

From Rockstar (2011). Composed by A.R. Rahman, lyrics by Irshad Kamil, sung by Mohit Chauhan and Suzanne D'Mello. A quietly devastating love song about the gap between feeling someone and truly having them. "Jitna mehsoos karoon tumko, utna hi paa bhi loon." The more you feel them, the more you want to hold on. One of the softer tracks on an otherwise intense album, sitting somewhere between Sadda Haq and silence.

  

Khaabon Ke Parindey

From Zindagi Na Milegi Dobara (2011). Composed by Shankar-Ehsaan-Loy, lyrics by Javed Akhtar, sung by Mohit Chauhan and Alyssa Mendonsa. A song about waking up mid-life and deciding to let go. "Fikrein jo thi, peechhe reh gayi", the worries you carried have been left behind. Where Tum Ho is about holding on, this one is about letting go. The same bossanova groove that slows Tum Ho down somehow sets this one free.
 

"We weren't going to put this out. It was just for the room. But some things are too good to stay inside a room."

A note from the Talent Department

One of the most surprising artistic connections I have found at Artium has been with Shrey. We never planned it. Our creative worlds just kept colliding when we least expected. Over the years, the two of us have quietly become one of those pairings people ask for when there is a stage involved.

Performing is its own kind of thrill. But performing with a friend is something else entirely. Tum Ho x Khaabon Ke Parindey came from that place. Here's to more such experiments.
