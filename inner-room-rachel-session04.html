What happened in Session 04 of The Nuances of Western Vocals by Rachel Dohling.

Session Mentor

Rachel Dohling

Rachel Dohling is a vocalist with a rare command of the Western vocal tradition. As Category Manager, Western Vocals at Artium Academy, she brings both artistic depth and pedagogical precision to her teaching. She leads The Nuances of Western Vocals for the Artium Talent Cohort, a track built on the belief that great singing is not guesswork but mastery of one's own anatomy.

Rachel began the session with a demonstration, letting the cohort hear and feel what vocal onset sounds like before she gave it a name. The difference between a phrase that begins cleanly and one that begins with too much force or too much air behind it. The cohort heard it immediately. What they did not yet know was that they had just heard the most important moment in any phrase.

A fraction of a second.

But that fraction shapes everything that follows.

A singer's tonal stability hinges on the initial millisecond when breath first meets the vocal folds. That is the onset. Rachel described it as the voice's first technical impression. It is far easier to start with the right coordination from the beginning than to try and fix a rocky phrase mid-song. The cohort was not content to just understand this as theory. They wanted to feel it.

Same phrase.
Started differently.
A completely different sound.

After the anatomy breakdown, each student selected a phrase from a song of their own choice, in diverse languages, and applied different onset types in real time. This gave the session a very immediate, physical quality. A phrase they had sung before became something to examine from the very first note. The cohort could hear the difference. More importantly, they felt it in their own voice.

What stayed in the room was exactly that. Rachel had walked them through the mechanics of how a voice actually begins a note, and then asked each of them to test it on a phrase from a song they already knew. In the span of a single note, something clicked. They could feel the difference between a clean onset and a forced one. A fraction of a second. But that fraction shapes everything that follows. Once a phrase begins wrong, it pulls the rest of the line with it. Once they felt that, not heard it described but felt it, the guesswork stopped.

 

"There's nothing like watching that lightbulb moment when a student realizes a beautiful tone isn't an abstract trick; it is just pure coordination between breath and muscle. Once they understand the mechanics of their own anatomy, the guesswork stops and they start actively steering their voice. Watching that shift happen is incredible."

Rachel Dohling
